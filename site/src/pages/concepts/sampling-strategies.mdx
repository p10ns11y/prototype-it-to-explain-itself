# Sampling Strategies

Temperature is the most famous control, but on its own it is blunt.

This reference explains the two most important production techniques (Top-k and nucleus / Top-p) and why the combination of temperature + top-p is the practical default in almost every deployed LLM (including Grok).

## The Core Problem

A model produces a probability distribution over the vocabulary at every step.

- High temperature → more random / creative.
- Low temperature → more deterministic / repetitive.

But temperature alone does not prevent the model from putting tiny probability mass on a huge number of bad tokens.

## Top-k Sampling

Keep only the k most likely tokens and renormalize.

**Intuition table** (simplified):

| k   | Behavior                     | Risk                     |
|-----|------------------------------|--------------------------|
| 1   | Greedy / very safe           | Repetitive, boring       |
| 10  | Good balance for most tasks  | Can still pick weird tail tokens |
| 40–50 | Common production default  | More diversity           |

## Nucleus Sampling (Top-p)

Keep the smallest set of tokens whose cumulative probability exceeds p.

This is dynamic — on some steps it may keep 5 tokens, on others 200.

This is generally preferred over fixed Top-k because it adapts to the shape of the distribution.

## Practical Defaults (2024–2025)

Most teams ship something like:

- Temperature 0.7–1.0
- Top-p 0.9–0.95
- (Sometimes Top-k 40–50 as a secondary cap)

The combination gives controllable creativity while cutting off the long tail of low-probability garbage.
